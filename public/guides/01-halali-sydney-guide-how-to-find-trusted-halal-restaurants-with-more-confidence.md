## FAQ

### 1. Is a halal tag on a general delivery app enough?

Not usually. A tag can help you discover a restaurant, but it rarely explains whether the whole restaurant is full-halal, whether the kitchen handles non-halal food, or how the listing was checked. Use the tag as a starting point, then verify standards, kitchen practice, and review logic before you order.

### 2. What is the first thing I should check in Sydney?

Check the platform standard before you check the menu. If the platform cannot explain what halal means inside its system, everything after that becomes harder to trust. Start with definitions, mixed-kitchen policy, and review method, then compare restaurants.

### 3. Why does mixed-kitchen information matter so much?

Because many Muslim users are not only checking ingredients. They are trying to understand the broader kitchen environment and whether it matches their own trust threshold. If a platform never addresses mixed-kitchen risk, users are left filling in the gap themselves.

### 4. Does "full-halal" mean the same thing as "halal options"?

No. "Halal options" usually means some menu items are suitable, while "full-halal" signals a stronger whole-restaurant standard. That difference matters because a trust-first user is not only comparing dishes. They are comparing operating models and confidence levels.

### 5. Why is re-checking important?

Restaurant information changes. Certificates can expire, suppliers can change, and operational details can drift over time. A platform that talks openly about re-checking gives users a stronger reason to trust that today's listing still reflects today's reality.

### 6. Why does Sydney-specific availability matter?

Clear availability language reduces confusion. If a platform says it is operating in Sydney, users know they are reading a live-city use case instead of a generic national claim. That clarity matters because trust is stronger when scope is stated directly, not implied.

### 7. Should I care about support and complaint handling too?

Yes. Trust does not stop at the restaurant card. If something looks wrong or feels inconsistent, users need a clear path for raising concerns. A platform that feels accountable after the order is often more trustworthy before the order as well.
